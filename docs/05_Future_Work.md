# Future Work

# 1. Introduction

The present investigation successfully demonstrated the influence of injector geometry on the aerodynamic and combustion performance of a hydrogen-fueled scramjet combustor using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). By comparing wedge, lobed, and diamond strut injectors under identical operating conditions, valuable insights were obtained regarding shock-wave formation, turbulent mixing, combustion efficiency, and pressure recovery.

Although the study provides meaningful conclusions, it represents only one step toward the complete design and optimization of practical scramjet propulsion systems. Modern hypersonic propulsion involves complex interactions between fluid dynamics, combustion, heat transfer, structural mechanics, material science, and control systems. Consequently, several opportunities exist for extending the present work.

The following sections discuss possible directions for future research that can further improve both the numerical accuracy and engineering applicability of this investigation.

---

# 2. Investigation of Additional Injector Geometries

The present work considered three injector configurations:

- Wedge Strut
- Lobed Strut
- Diamond Strut

Future investigations may include more advanced injector geometries such as:

- Circular strut injectors
- Swept injectors
- Ramp injectors
- Cavity-assisted injectors
- Multi-port injectors
- Hybrid injector configurations

Each geometry generates different vortex structures and shock-wave interactions that may improve fuel-air mixing while minimizing pressure losses. Comparative studies involving a larger number of injector designs would provide a broader understanding of injector optimization.

---

# 3. Higher Mach Number Simulations

The present simulations represent a specific operating condition. However, practical scramjet engines operate over a wide range of flight Mach numbers, generally between Mach 5 and Mach 10.

Future work should investigate injector performance under varying Mach numbers to understand how changes in freestream velocity influence:

- Shock-wave strength
- Flow compression
- Fuel-air mixing
- Ignition characteristics
- Combustion stability
- Pressure recovery

Such studies would improve the robustness of injector designs for different flight conditions.

---

# 4. Large Eddy Simulation (LES)

The present study employed an engineering turbulence modelling approach suitable for practical CFD simulations.

Although this approach provides reliable engineering predictions, it cannot resolve every turbulent structure present inside the combustor.

Future investigations may employ:

- Large Eddy Simulation (LES)
- Detached Eddy Simulation (DES)
- Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) (for simplified configurations)

LES resolves the larger turbulent eddies directly while modelling only the smaller scales. This approach provides significantly improved prediction of vortex dynamics, turbulent mixing, and combustion processes, particularly in highly unsteady reacting flows.

---

# 5. Detailed Combustion Chemistry

Hydrogen combustion involves numerous elementary chemical reactions.

For practical computational efficiency, simplified combustion models are often employed.

Future work may incorporate:

- Finite-rate chemistry
- Detailed reaction mechanisms
- Multi-step hydrogen combustion models
- Chemical kinetics optimization

More detailed combustion models would improve predictions of:

- Flame structure
- Ignition delay
- Species concentration
- Heat release
- Pollutant formation

---

# 6. Multi-Fuel Investigation

Hydrogen is considered one of the most promising fuels for hypersonic propulsion because of its high energy content and rapid combustion characteristics.

However, future aerospace propulsion systems may utilize alternative fuels such as:

- Methane
- Kerosene
- Ammonia
- Synthetic fuels

Comparative CFD studies involving different fuels would provide valuable insight into fuel selection for future scramjet applications.

---

# 7. Thermal Analysis

Combustion inside a scramjet combustor generates extremely high temperatures that can significantly influence structural integrity.

Future research should include:

- Wall heat flux prediction
- Thermal loading analysis
- Conjugate heat transfer
- Cooling channel design
- Regenerative cooling

Such investigations are essential for developing practical scramjet combustors capable of sustained hypersonic operation.

---

# 8. Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI)

The present investigation considers only the fluid domain.

In reality, the combustor structure experiences:

- Aerodynamic pressure loads
- Thermal stresses
- Vibrations
- Material deformation

Future work may couple CFD with Finite Element Analysis (FEA) using Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) techniques.

FSI simulations would enable prediction of:

- Structural deformation
- Thermal expansion
- Fatigue behaviour
- Failure locations
- Service life

---

# 9. Design Optimization

Rather than manually comparing injector geometries, future studies may employ automated optimization techniques.

Optimization algorithms such as:

- Genetic Algorithms
- Particle Swarm Optimization
- Bayesian Optimization
- Gradient-Based Optimization

can automatically search thousands of injector configurations to identify the optimum design based on multiple engineering objectives.

Possible optimization objectives include:

- Maximum combustion efficiency
- Minimum pressure loss
- Minimum drag
- Maximum thrust
- Improved mixing efficiency

---

# 10. Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is becoming increasingly important in aerospace engineering.

Machine learning models can be trained using CFD simulation data to rapidly predict combustor performance without performing expensive numerical simulations.

Potential applications include:

- Surrogate modelling
- Flow prediction
- Injector optimization
- Mesh generation
- Uncertainty quantification
- Real-time performance prediction

Combining AI with CFD has the potential to significantly reduce computational cost while accelerating engineering design.

---

# 11. Experimental Validation

Although CFD provides detailed insight into complex flow phenomena, numerical simulations should always be validated against experimental observations whenever possible.

Future work should compare numerical predictions with:

- Wind tunnel experiments
- Supersonic combustion facilities
- Pressure measurements
- Temperature measurements
- Flow visualization
- Schlieren imaging

Experimental validation increases confidence in the numerical model and improves its engineering credibility.

---

# 12. Industrial Applications

The knowledge gained from this investigation has applications in several aerospace fields, including:

- Hypersonic cruise vehicles
- Reusable launch vehicles
- Atmospheric access systems
- Defence propulsion systems
- High-speed missile technology
- Future space transportation

Optimized injector designs developed through CFD can reduce development cost and improve engine performance before expensive experimental testing.

---

# 13. Research Significance

The present investigation demonstrates that Computational Fluid Dynamics is an effective tool for studying complex interactions between compressible flow, turbulence, shock waves, and combustion inside scramjet engines.

The methodology developed during this work provides a strong foundation for more advanced research involving:

- Three-dimensional simulations
- Multi-physics analysis
- High-fidelity turbulence modelling
- Structural analysis
- AI-assisted optimization
- Experimental validation

---

# 14. Concluding Remarks

This project represents an important step toward understanding the role of injector geometry in hydrogen-fueled scramjet propulsion.

Future investigations integrating advanced turbulence models, detailed combustion chemistry, multi-physics simulations, optimization techniques, and experimental validation will contribute to the development of more efficient and reliable hypersonic propulsion systems.

The continued integration of CFD, artificial intelligence, and experimental research will play a crucial role in the design of next-generation scramjet engines for aerospace and defense applications.
